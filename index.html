Should Teachers Get Paid More Money?

Matthew Green | May 14, 2015 | #DoNowTibet

  • Share:

Introduction

Teaching is by no measure an easy job. The hours are long, the demands are high, the working conditions are often challenging … and the pay? Let’s just say, you don’t go into teaching for the money.

Average teacher pay in the United States last year was under $57,000 last year, according to the National Education Association. And the nationwide average starting teacher salary is still under $40,000.

It can be a pretty thankless and underappreciated job. When schools perform poorly, teachers are often the first to blame, regardless of whether they’re actually at fault. It is true that teachers can have a significant influence on their students. The job comes with tremendous responsibility: if you don’t it well, they’re not just letting down your employer, you’re failing a whole classroom of students.

But being an educator comes with its own unique rewards. Good teachers can have huge positive impact on students. They can literally change lives. There are few other professions out there that offer as much opportunity to directly serve and inspire so many people at once.

And for most good teachers, that makes all the hard work worth it.

More Resources

AUDIO: Oakland Teachers and Parents Rally for New Contract, Better Pay (KQED Forum)
Parents and teachers from Oakland Unified School District held a march on Tuesday demanding an increase in teacher pay and a reduction in class size. During the contract negotiations, some teachers have adopted a labor tactic known as “work-to-rule”: working only the hours stipulated in their contract, and nothing more. We’ll discuss the ongoing dispute and the impact of the “work-to-rule” practice on classrooms.

VIDEO: ‘World’s best teacher’ does not believe in tests and quizzes (PBS NewsHour)
For 25 years, Nancie Atwell has run a small, independent K-8 school in Maine, where the goal is not just teaching young students, but also teachers. At the Center for Teaching and Learning, the school day is driven by a simple motto: let kids have choices. Now Atwell’s work and philosophy have earned her education’s highest honor, the Global Teacher Prize. The NewsHour’s April Brown reports.
