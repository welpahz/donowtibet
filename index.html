Does China have the right to change Tibet's cultural identity?

NTH@C | May 14, 2015 | #DoNowTibet |

Photo by Intel Free Press

To respond to this discussion board, you can comment below or tweet your response. Be sure to begin your tweet with #DoNowTibet and #boydbence.

Do Now

1. Do you think China has the right to mold Tibet and change Tibet's cultural identity?

2. What actions should the international community take to aid this conflict?

3. If you were in charge of negotiations between Tibet and China, what course of action would you propose?

Introduction

China and Tibet have a complicated relationship. Tibet was originally its own independent nation until the early 1900’s when it was then occupied by Great Britain and later China. In 1912, Tibet reclaimed its Independence.

In 1950, the newly formed People's Republic of China decided to invade Tibet. With the stability that has been reestablished in Beijing, Mao went to assert China's right to rule over tibet. The People's Liberation Army (PLA) came in a had a swift and total defeat of the Tibetan army. Then china wrote the “Seventeen Point Agreement” making Tibet a new region of China.

China started to do land redistribution in Tibet. Monasteries were redistributed to the peasants. China wanted to destroy the power base of the wealthy buddhist in the Tibetan society. The Tibetan monks led an uprising that started in 1956 and went on until 1959. They used guerrilla tactics in attempt to drive out the chinese. The chinese responded by obliterating villages and monasteries. The Dalai Lama was also exiled in 1959 and forced to go to india and govern from there. These 3 years left 86,000 Tibetans dead. In the next 30 years, up to 1.2 million Tibetans were killed with at least 400,000 confirmed.

Before China had occupied Tibet, it is estimated that there were over 6,000 monasteries. Now it is said there are only 8. The communist Chinese only allowed a few famous and well known monasteries to physically survive. The rest were destroyed. Along with monasteries, other parts of Tibetan culture were destroyed. Many statues were also destroyed in the Chinese invasion including a huge 30-foot Buddhist statue.

The Chinese government soon realized that the only way to seize control over Tibet and its people is through its religion. When the 10th Panchen Lama died, the number two in Tibetan Buddhism behind the Dalai Lama, the search for the next Panchen Lama began. Tibetan Buddhist believe in reincarnation and that the Panchen Lama is just reincarnated into a different body. The Dalai Lama is in charge of finding the new Panchen Lama. After 6 years of searching, Gendhun Choekyi Nyima was declared to be the 11th Panchen Lama. Just 3 days later, him and his family had gone missing. Later that year, the chinese government that they had found the Panchen Lama themselves and enthroned Gyaltsen Norbu. In a 2001 press release, China claimed its handpicked boy lama was loyal to the Communist Party. He is quoted by China's news agency, Xinhua, as saying "I have felt the greatness of the Communist Party of China and the warmth of the socialist family." The Xinhua report said that the Beijing-selected boy is intelligent, a good student, and is able to recite more than 400 pages of sutras, adding that he has an unusual understanding of the sutras. With the Dalai Lama now 79 years old, many fear for what may happen after he dies. The new Dalai Lama which will be chosen by the Panchen Lama could end up allowing the Chinese government to gain control of Tibet. Worst of all, a major part of the Tibetan culture will be lost.

Throughout the Tibet and China conflict, there have been shifts of power over the land of Tibet. But to present day, many aspects of the Tibetan culture and beliefs have been destroyed by the Chinese communists and the future is unclear for those who still believe in Tibet.
